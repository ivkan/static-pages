Bottom line: The calibration engine has done everything the formula allows. The remaining gap to 66.2% — and to any higher target — is caused by a structural constraint in the pricing model itself, not by incorrect parameters.
The core constraint
The formula produces one price. Reality produces many.
For any given city and distance range, the formula outputs a single fixed price. But historical data shows that trips in the same city, at the same distance, were charged very different amounts — sometimes 5× apart. No amount of parameter tuning can make one number match a range of numbers.

Key insight: A city correction factor (rate_adjustment) can shift the formula's single output up or down — but it cannot split it into multiple values. The spread within each city is invisible to the formula.
Statistical evidence
High price variance = low accuracy. Always.
Across all 678 cities, the correlation between price variability and prediction accuracy is −0.325. Cities where historical prices jump around unpredictably are exactly the cities the formula performs worst on. This is not coincidence — it is the mathematical consequence of fitting one value to a distribution.

What would change this
Three questions only the business can answer
The data analysis points to three possible explanations for the price spread. Each has a different implication for whether 90%+ accuracy is achievable at all.
1
Were historical prices affected by manual dispatcher overrides?
If dispatchers could manually adjust prices — discounts for loyal clients, surge pricing for last-minute bookings, event premiums — then the historical "TotalPrice" does not reflect the formula at all. No calibration can match prices set by human judgment.
If YES → 90% accuracy is impossible on this dataset. A new clean dataset is needed.
2
Did time-of-day or demand influence pricing?
The dataset records only a recalculation date (2026-02-13 for all trips), not the actual booking date or time. If prices were higher on Friday evenings, during events, or in peak seasons, the formula cannot capture this without a time dimension in the data.
If YES → Providing actual trip dates could enable time-based analysis and improve accuracy.
3
Were some trips priced under a different formula version?
Canberra and similar cities where the formula systematically over- or under-prices all trips in the same direction suggests the formula rules may have changed over time. Trips priced under different rules would corrupt the calibration signal.
If YES → Filtering the dataset to a known date range could significantly raise the ceiling.
Immediate request: Please provide the actual trip booking dates. With dates, time-of-day and day-of-week analysis becomes possible — this is the single highest-impact data point that could change the ceiling estimate.
